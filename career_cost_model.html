Career & cost model — driven by promotion pace

Unlike the first model (which assumes where staff sit), this one derives it: staff are appointed at the bottom of each grade, gain one increment a year, and are promoted after a set number of years. Crucially, that interacts with each university's grade length — short grades make staff hit their ceiling sooner. A representative permanent career starts at age 28 (Lecturer) and, on the middle pace, reaches Professor at 48, matching the sector average. Professorial pay itself is excluded.

Assumptions & limits
• Promotion pace sets years-in-grade; the within-grade spread is derived from that vs each university's grade length (staff climb one point/yr, then sit at the ceiling until promoted).
• Salaries above spine point 51 (local non-professorial spine extensions, which differ by institution) use each institution's own published values.
• Career start age 28; one representative path; everyone promotes on schedule — real workforces have non-promotion and attrition, so the trajectory is illustrative; the cost tab instead weights by Exeter's actual HESA grade mix (E&R; SL/AP split 50/50).
• Contribution points modelled as a share of ceiling-stuck staff holding one permanent point; they are the single softest input (no sector data) — refine when Exeter's own data is available.
• Salaries = verified 2025-26 national spine (+2% optional). On-costs: NI 15% above £5,000, USS 14.5%, levy 0.5%. Excludes professorial pay, London weighting (none in the included universities), transition costs.
• Sector evidence puts L→SL at ~5–7 yrs and Prof at ~48; upper transitions are weakly evidenced.
