High pay at Exeter — five years of £100,000+

Every annual report lists the number of staff paid £100,000 or more (basic pay, excluding employer pension contributions and the Vice-Chancellor, who is reported separately). This page collects that table from the last five published reports — six years of data, since each report also restates the previous year — and asks the obvious question: is the growth just pay awards pushing people over a fixed £100k line, or is something else going on?

What this means

Two things worth holding together. First, most of the headline tripling is arithmetic: the top of Exeter's professorial ladder sits just below £100k, so each national pay award pushes a large, pre-existing cohort across a fixed line — that is a fact about the threshold, not about hiring. Second, what remains after adjusting for awards is still real growth of roughly 30% in five years in the £100k+ (2019/20 terms) population — over the same period in which two voluntary severance rounds removed ~700 posts and ~150 more are proposed for cuts. The high-pay population grew while the workforce shrank.

Is Exeter an outlier? Every university publishes the same table in its annual report (an Office for Students requirement), so this comparison is extendable to the whole Russell Group. If you can share peers' annual reports (or just their higher-paid-staff pages), the same charts can be built comparatively — the natural normaliser is £100k+ staff per 1,000 academic staff.

Sources, method & caveats
• Source: University of Exeter Annual Reports and Financial Statements 2020/21–2024/25 (Note 7, "Higher paid staff"), archived in sources/. Each report also restates the prior year; restated and originally-reported figures agree in every overlapping year, so the series is internally consistent.
• Figures are basic pay excluding employer pension (except salary-sacrifice), excluding the Vice-Chancellor, and — per the OfS Accounts Direction — excluding staff who joined or left part-way through the year (stated from 2022/23; earlier reports use the same note format).
• The award-adjusted line uprates the £100,000 threshold by the national (JNCHES) pay award as applied at £100k-level spine points: 2020/21 0%, 2021/22 +1.5%, 2022/23 +3% (Aug 2022) +2% (Feb 2023 interim) — Exeter's own 2022/23 report attributes that year's jump to these two increases — 2023/24 +3% (remainder of the 5% 2023 award), 2024/25 +2.5% (verified against published spine values). Cumulative: £100,000 in 2019/20 ≈ £112,600 in 2024/25. Counts above mid-band thresholds are interpolated linearly within the £5k band.
• Headcount, not FTE, in the charts (FTE shown in the table); part-year staff excluded per the accounts direction.
• The professorial-ladder explanation for the £100–110k bulge is corroborated on the Professorial tab: Exeter's ladder tops out at £105,062–£111,459, exactly where the 161-person 2024/25 bulge sits.
