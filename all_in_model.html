How this compares

What this is — and what it isn't

This dashboard estimates how much of Exeter's academic pay bill is attributable to its pay structure rather than to paying people more on the same national spine. It does so by applying Exeter's own workforce to other Russell Group universities' published pay structures, so the only thing that varies is the system, not the people.

The figures are indicative, not precise. They rest on reasoned assumptions — promotion pace, contribution-point uptake, the Senior-Lecturer/Associate-Professor split, professorial award rates — for which there is little hard sector data. The value here is as a yardstick for the order of magnitude, and for testing whether the conclusion survives plausible changes in those assumptions (it does). Treat any single number as "roughly this many", not "exactly this many".

Headline findings

What drives the difference — and the biggest levers

Exeter doesn't have to copy any one institution. The gap comes from a few specific structural choices, in roughly descending order of impact:

  1. Where each grade starts (entry point) — the single biggest lever. Exeter's Lecturer grade begins at spine point 34 (matched only by Bristol); most peers start at 28–32, and Nottingham and Warwick at 27. A higher floor applies to everyone, every year, and compounds over a career.
  2. Grade length — Exeter's grades are short (3 / 5 / 3 normal increments), so staff reach the ceiling quickly and park there until promotion. Longer grades (e.g. Nottingham 11 / 9 / 8) keep people climbing rather than sitting at the maximum — cheaper for the same promotion timing.
  3. The contribution-point tail — Warwick has none and is among the cheapest; Exeter's Associate-Professor grade carries six discretionary points above the ceiling. The softest input, but a real lever.
  4. Ceiling height and overlap — high ceilings, heavy overlap with the grade above, and expensive local points beyond spine 51 pull the average up (this is why Leeds is dear despite a reasonable entry point).

Gaps in the spine (skipped points) are a red herring — they slightly raise cost by accelerating progression to the ceiling, not lower it. And one honest caveat: a "structural saving" largely means paying new staff less at given career stages — the recruitment cost Exeter's high floor was presumably meant to avoid; it is not free of consequences, only free of compulsory redundancy. The cleanest lever the data supports is to lower entry points and lengthen the lower grades: it captures most of the gap, is less fraught than cutting the discretionary tail, and (unlike redundancy) touches no one currently employed.

Why the headline is a minimum — research-only and professional-services staff

The headline covers E&R and teaching-only academics plus the professoriate. Two whole groups are left out, and both point the same way: research-only staff (bounded below) and professional-services staff, whose grade structures differ between universities in exactly the same way as academic ones but whose scales and staffing are not yet compiled here. Whatever they would add, they only add — so the headline figures are best read as the minimum structural difference.

This model covers the three academic grades and the professoriate. It does not include research-only staff (research associates and fellows), who mostly sit on the grade below Lecturer. The same structural logic applies, but the budget effect depends on who pays: for internally funded posts (bridging funding, core fellowships) the cost falls on Exeter directly, so the saving is real and of the same kind; for externally funded posts (the majority) the grant pays the salary, so a dearer structure is not a direct core-budget saving — but it still raises Exeter's co-funding share of unrecovered full economic cost and makes its grants more expensive per post. Quantifying it would need the internal/external split, the funder mix and peers' research-grade scales, most of which are not in the current data. Flagged as a likely additional saving, deliberately left out of the headline figures.

A rough bound on its size: of Exeter's 780 full-time research-only staff (HESA 2024/25), 545 sit in the Lecturer and Senior-Lecturer salary bands that this model can price. Running just those 545 through the same structural comparison gives roughly £2m/yr vs the mean of the modelled systems and £4m/yr vs the cheapest (salary only; ~£3–5m with on-costs) — on top of the headline. The remaining 230 sit on the research grade below Lecturer, for which peers' scales are not yet collected. Because external funders pay most research salaries, only part of this would land on Exeter's core budget — treat it as scale, not a forecast.

Does the model add up? (calibration)

A fair question is whether the modelled bill is the right size at all. The check is to compare the modelled Exeter figure (with on-costs switched on) against the academic staff costs in Exeter's published financial statements. The modelled population is deliberately narrower than the accounts — full-time staff in the three main academic grades plus the professoriate, excluding part-time staff, research-only staff and non-academic staff — so the modelled figure should come to well under the accounts' total, with the gap explained by those exclusions.

On the central assumptions the modelled bill is ≈£106m in salary, or ≈£137m including employer on-costs — about 35% of the £396.6m total staff costs in the University's 2024/25 Annual Report (year to 31 July 2025). The remaining ~65% is exactly what the model deliberately leaves out: professional-services staff, research-only staff, part-time staff, the grades below Lecturer, and non-salary staff costs such as severance. That proportion is the right size for the population modelled — the model passes the check. The accounts' activity split (Note 9) tightens it further: staff costs in academic colleges were £186.4m, so the modelled ≈£137m is about 73% of the academic-colleges pay bill, with the residual being part-time staff, the grades below Lecturer and college-based professional staff; externally-funded research staff sit mostly in the separate £61.1m research-grants line, which is why the model's research-only exclusion barely touches the academic-colleges figure. The accounts also name the drivers of staff-cost growth — the national pay award, the employer NI increase and living-wage adoption — which are the same components the model's on-cost line carries (NI 15% above £5,000, USS 14.5%, levy 0.5%).

Figures from the University of Exeter Annual Report and Financial Statements 2024/25. One health warning when reading those accounts: the headline surplus fell from £186.5m to £1.3m, but £177.7m of the prior-year figure was a one-off USS pension provision release — accounting noise, not trading. The honest year-on-year line is the operating surplus: £22.5m down to £8.2m. This site deliberately uses only the operating line.

What the accounts say about scale

The structural difference modelled here — roughly £5–9m/yr in salary, £7–11m/yr with on-costs (vs the mean and the lowest system respectively) — is of the same order as Exeter's entire 2024/25 operating surplus (£8.2m, down from £22.5m), and roughly half the ~£20m full-year effect of both voluntary severance rounds (TERS and TERS 2, ~450 staff) combined — at steady state, and without redundancy. The accounts show a solvent, liquid institution (net cash, USS in surplus, a clean going-concern opinion) with a genuinely thinning margin driven by falling international income: real pressure, met so far almost entirely through staff reduction. Pay structure is a comparable-scale lever on the same cost line that has not been part of the public conversation. The redundancy lever also has a price the structural one does not: the accounts show £10.0m of compensation for loss of office for 427 posts in 2024/25 alone (£8.8m for 268 posts the year before) — a structural change grandfathers existing staff and pays no severance. And on the pay the site does not model: remuneration of the 16 key management personnel was £3.85m including pension (~£240k per post) — the executive-pay scrutiny this site keeps flagging as the other half of the picture.
