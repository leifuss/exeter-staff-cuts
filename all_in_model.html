What this is — and what it isn't

This dashboard estimates how much of Exeter's academic pay bill is attributable to its pay structure rather than to paying people more on the same national spine. It does so by applying Exeter's own workforce to other Russell Group universities' published pay structures, so the only thing that varies is the system, not the people.

The figures are indicative, not precise. They rest on reasoned assumptions — promotion pace, contribution-point uptake, the Senior-Lecturer/Associate-Professor split, professorial award rates — for which there is little hard sector data. The value here is as a yardstick for the order of magnitude, and for testing whether the conclusion survives plausible changes in those assumptions (it does). Treat any single number as "roughly this many", not "exactly this many".

Headline findings

What drives the difference — and the biggest levers

Exeter doesn't have to copy any one institution. The gap comes from a few specific structural choices, in roughly descending order of impact:

  1. Where each grade starts (entry point) — the single biggest lever. Exeter's Lecturer grade begins at spine point 34 (matched only by Bristol); most peers start at 30–32, and Nottingham, Sheffield and Warwick at 27–28. A higher floor applies to everyone, every year, and compounds over a career.
  2. Grade length — Exeter's grades are short (3 / 5 / 3 normal increments), so staff reach the ceiling quickly and park there until promotion. Longer grades (e.g. Nottingham 11 / 9 / 8) keep people climbing rather than sitting at the maximum — cheaper for the same promotion timing.
  3. The contribution-point tail — Warwick has none and is among the cheapest; Exeter's Associate-Professor grade carries six discretionary points above the ceiling. The softest input, but a real lever.
  4. Ceiling height and overlap — high ceilings, heavy overlap with the grade above, and expensive local points beyond spine 51 pull the average up (this is why Leeds is dear despite a reasonable entry point).

Gaps in the spine (skipped points) are a red herring — they slightly raise cost by accelerating progression to the ceiling, not lower it. And one honest caveat: a "structural saving" largely means paying new staff less at given career stages — the recruitment cost Exeter's high floor was presumably meant to avoid; it is not free of consequences, only free of compulsory redundancy. The cleanest lever the data supports is to lower entry points and lengthen the lower grades: it captures most of the gap, is less fraught than cutting the discretionary tail, and (unlike redundancy) touches no one currently employed.

Research-only staff — likely further saving, not yet modelled

This model covers the three academic grades and the professoriate. It does not include research-only staff (research associates and fellows), who mostly sit on the grade below Lecturer. The same structural logic applies, but the budget effect depends on who pays: for internally funded posts (bridging funding, core fellowships) the cost falls on Exeter directly, so the saving is real and of the same kind; for externally funded posts (the majority) the grant pays the salary, so a dearer structure is not a direct core-budget saving — but it still raises Exeter's co-funding share of unrecovered full economic cost and makes its grants more expensive per post. Quantifying it would need the internal/external split, the funder mix and Exeter's research-grade scales, none of which are in the current data. Flagged as a likely additional saving, deliberately left out of the headline figures.
