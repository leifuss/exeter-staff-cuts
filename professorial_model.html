Professorial pay — Russell Group comparators

Professors are paid differently from other academics: no automatic increments — pay rises only through a discretionary review (typically every two years), and only some succeed. The model assumes, from national statistics, that the average professor is promoted to the lowest professorial grade at about age 48 and works to retirement at 67 (~9 biennial reviews) — individual careers will of course vary widely. You can tweak how likely each review is to grant a rise (the award rate), which is the main cost lever here; actual pay steps follow each university's local, non-UCU professorial scales — the national spine plays no role here. What differs between universities is the floor each ladder starts at (Sheffield £71.6k … Exeter £78.2k), the size of each step (Southampton's rungs are ~1% apart, Exeter's ~3%) and the number of rungs — and because progression is discretionary and slow, the starting floor does most of the work. Band 3 (rare, by exception) is excluded; weighted by Exeter's professoriate applied to every structure, and kept separate from the non-professorial models.

What the accounts corroborate

Exeter's 2024/25 Annual Report reports 405 staff earning £100,000 or more (basic pay, excluding employer pension) — with a striking bulge of 161 people in the £105,000–£110,000 band, up from 23 the year before. That band sits exactly at the top rungs of Exeter's professorial ladder (£105,062 / £108,214), consistent with a large cohort of long-serving professors parked near the ladder's upper end after years of biennial awards. Two implications: the model's professorial layer is pointing at a real, large population, and any structural saving on the professoriate would be concentrated among these ~400 highest-paid staff — the group best able to absorb slower progression, and the opposite end of the pay scale from the posts proposed for cuts.
