Academic pay structures — Russell Group & comparator universities

Why this site exists: the Exeter cuts — and a question the official case skips

In June 2026 the University of Exeter began consulting on cutting the equivalent of around 150 full-time posts (more than 500 staff notified at risk), falling heavily on the humanities and social sciences. This site asks a question the official case has not: how does Exeter's academic pay structure compare with other Russell Group universities'?

Built from universities' own published pay scales, it finds Exeter's structure the most expensive of the eleven Russell Group systems modelled (Manchester not yet covered). Converging on the Russell Group median structure — the principal benchmark throughout — would save roughly £7–9m a year: comparable to Exeter's entire 2024/25 operating surplus (£8.2m), recurring, and with no severance bill. It is convergence with the middle of the peer group, not a race to the bottom. On that evidence the scale of the cuts looks less like a necessity than a choice. (The UCU reports Exeter's own modelling shows the deficit closing by 2029 without compulsory redundancies.)

Two things this is not. It is not a claim that any individual is overpaid: every university here is on the same national pay spine, so a given point pays the same everywhere — only the design of the grades differs. And it can't be read in isolation — realigning structure saves only gradually (see the Transition tab), and executive and senior-management pay deserves equal scrutiny (that data isn't compiled here). The pay scales are public and the data is downloadable, so anyone can check, challenge or extend this.

What this shows — and what it doesn't
  • Every university here uses the same national pay spine: a given spine point pays the same salary everywhere. Only the structure — where each grade starts, how long it runs, what sits above its ceiling — differs.
  • We price Exeter's own current staff on each university's structure, so the only thing that changes is the pay system, not the people.
  • On that like-for-like test, Exeter's structure is the most expensive of the eleven systems we could model. The principal benchmark is the Russell Group median — the middle of the peer group; the cheapest system is shown too, but as a bound, not a proposal.
  • The difference is comparable in scale to the proposed ~150-post cuts — but a structural change phases in over roughly five years and saves mostly by paying future staff less. It is not cash in the bank this year.
  • The figures are a minimum: they cover only E&R and teaching-only academics plus the professoriate. Research-only staff (~780 full-time) and professional-services staff — whose grade structures differ in the same way — are not counted, and would likely add to the saving.
  • This is a comparison, not a costed alternative plan — and not a claim that anyone is overpaid.
Jargon in plain English. E&R = Education & Research staff (the standard teaching-and-research academic contract) · teaching-only = education-focused academic contracts · FTE = full-time equivalent (two half-time staff = 1 FTE) · HESA = the Higher Education Statistics Agency, the official source for staff numbers · spine point = a numbered step on the national salary ladder, worth the same £ at every university · on-costs = employer National Insurance, pension (USS) and levy paid on top of salary · contribution points = discretionary extra steps above a grade's normal ceiling.
These are indicative figures. The pay scales themselves are exact and public; the modelling on top — promotion pace, contribution-point uptake, the senior-lecturer/associate-professor split, professorial award rates — rests on assumptions for which there is limited hard sector data. Treat every number as "roughly this many", not "exactly": a yardstick for the order of magnitude, useful mainly for testing whether a conclusion survives plausible changes in the assumptions.
Please check it yourself — don't just take this analysis. The underlying data is provided in machine-readable form in this project's GitHub repository (grades.json, grades.csv, national_spine.csv, cost_model_results.csv) precisely so others can re-run, challenge or extend it. This analysis has limitations and is better for more eyes — corrections and alternative approaches are welcome.

Coverage & scope. Built from the published scales of 17 universities (13 in the active comparison; 11 modelled as a whole pay bill). All on the national spine, so the salary at each point is identical and only the structure differs. The active set is Russell Group universities in England and Wales. Kept in the data but out of the comparison: the Scottish universities (Edinburgh, Glasgow, Aberdeen — different student-funding model) and non-Russell-Group comparators (e.g. Bath). Excluded by design: Oxford, Cambridge, the London institutions and Northern Ireland (distinct labour markets). Full provenance — each scale linked to the university's live page — is on the Sources tab.
