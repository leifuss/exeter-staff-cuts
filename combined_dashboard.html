Academic pay structures — Russell Group & comparator universities

Why this site exists: the Exeter cuts — and a question the official case skips

In June 2026 the University of Exeter began consulting on proposals to cut around 150 full-time posts (the UCU warns many more are at risk), citing falling tuition income, rising costs, underfunded research and a drop in international students. The reductions fall heavily on the humanities and social sciences. This site asks something the official case has not: how does Exeter's academic pay structure compare with other Russell Group universities'?

Built entirely from universities' own published pay scales, it finds that Exeter's structure is the most expensive of the English and Welsh Russell Group systems that could be modelled — and that the difference, applied to Exeter's own workforce, is of broadly the same order of magnitude as the saving the cuts are meant to deliver. On that evidence the scale of the cuts looks less like a necessity than a choice: pay structure is a large, comparable lever that has not been part of the public conversation. (As reported by the UCU, Exeter's own modelling is said to show the deficit closing by 2029 from reserves and voluntary measures, without compulsory redundancies — so alternatives plainly exist.)

Two things this is not. It is not a claim that any individual is overpaid: every university here is on the same national pay spine, so a given point pays the same everywhere — only the design of the grades differs. And it can't be read in isolation — realigning structure saves only gradually (see the Transition tab), and executive and senior-management pay deserves equal scrutiny (that data isn't compiled here). The pay scales are public and the data is downloadable, so anyone can check, challenge or extend this.

These are indicative figures. The pay scales themselves are exact and public; the modelling on top — promotion pace, contribution-point uptake, the senior-lecturer/associate-professor split, professorial award rates — rests on assumptions for which there is limited hard sector data. Treat every number as "roughly this many", not "exactly": a yardstick for the order of magnitude, useful mainly for testing whether a conclusion survives plausible changes in the assumptions.
Please check it yourself — don't just take this analysis. The underlying data is provided in machine-readable form (grades.json, grades.csv, national_spine.csv, cost_model_results.csv) precisely so others can re-run, challenge or extend it. This analysis has limitations and is better for more eyes — corrections and alternative approaches are welcome.

Coverage & scope. Built from the published scales of 16 universities (12 in the active comparison; 9 modelled as a whole pay bill). All on the national spine, so the salary at each point is identical and only the structure differs. The active set is Russell Group universities in England and Wales. Kept in the data but out of the comparison: the Scottish universities (Edinburgh, Glasgow, Aberdeen — different student-funding model) and non-Russell-Group comparators (e.g. Bath). Excluded by design: Oxford, Cambridge, the London institutions and Northern Ireland (distinct labour markets). Sheffield uses its 2004 grade architecture (its current scales are staff-login only). Full provenance — each scale linked to the university's live page — is on the Sources tab.
