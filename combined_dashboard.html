Russell Group academic pay — Exeter cost models

Why this matters

Exeter is cutting around 125 academic posts to balance its budget. Before accepting that as unavoidable, these models ask a prior question: does Exeter's unusual pay structure make its academic salary bill structurally higher than comparable universities' — independent of paying any individual more? If much of the gap is structural, then reforming the grade architecture (with no pay cuts) is an alternative lever to compulsory redundancy. On the central estimate, the structural gap is of the same order as the cuts themselves — which is why it is worth examining.

Six linked models estimating how much of Exeter's academic pay bill is attributable to its pay structure rather than to paying people more on the same national spine. Each applies Exeter's own workforce to other Russell Group universities' published structures, so only the system varies, not the people. Click a tab above, or a card below.

Read me — these are indicative figures. They rest on reasoned assumptions (promotion pace, contribution-point uptake, the SL/AP split, professorial award rates) for which there is little hard sector data. The value is as a yardstick for the order of magnitude and for testing whether the conclusion survives plausible changes (it does), not as precise accounting. Treat any number as "roughly this many", not "exactly".
The through-line across all six: Exeter sits at the expensive end of the modelled comparators at every level — most sharply in the non-professorial grades (where grade architecture is the lever) and more modestly among professors (where the discretionary award rate is the lever). On the all-in view Exeter is the single most expensive of the 10 modelled systems, and the structural difference is of the same order as the ~125 posts Exeter is cutting: about 84 equivalent average-salary posts vs the mean of the 10 modelled systems, ~134 vs the lowest (Nottingham), ~154 for the cheapest mix. Valued at the cheaper structures' own (lower) pay these are higher still (roughly ~88 / ~146 / ~169). These are equivalences for scale (saving ÷ average salary), not predicted cash savings or guaranteed jobs. Indicative.

Coverage: 13 non-professorial structures · 10 professorial · 10 all-in comparisons.
What drives the gap. The difference comes mainly from Exeter's high grade entry points (its Lecturer grade starts at spine point 34 vs 27–32 for most peers) and its short grades (staff top out fast and park at the ceiling), with the discretionary contribution-point tail a secondary lever. Gaps in the spine don't help — they slightly raise cost. The cleanest saving is lower entry points and longer lower grades, which (unlike redundancy) touch no current staff. Research-only staff (research associates/fellows) are not modelled here: internally-funded ones would show similar savings, externally-funded ones mainly as reduced grant co-funding — a likely additional saving left out of the headline. Which universities are included. The comparison covers English and Welsh Russell Group universities on the national pay spine for which pay data was available (13 non-professorial structures, 10 of them also professorial). Excluded by design: Oxford, Cambridge and the London institutions (incl. UCL), and Northern Ireland — distinct labour markets and cost bases; and the Scottish universities (Edinburgh, Glasgow, Aberdeen), which operate under a different student-funding model (their data is kept in the files but out of the comparison). Some other Russell Group universities are omitted simply because their pay data was not available. Sheffield is included on its 2004 grade architecture (a caveat noted in Sources).
